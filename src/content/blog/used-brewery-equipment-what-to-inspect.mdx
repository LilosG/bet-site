Used brewery equipment represents some of the best capital efficiency available to production operators. A well-maintained brewhouse from a working brewery costs a fraction of its replacement value and performs identically to a new system. The key word is well-maintained.

Knowing what to inspect — and what to walk away from — is the difference between a great purchase and an expensive problem.

## The General Principle: Wear Is Fine, Neglect Is Not

Production equipment that has been used looks used. That's expected and appropriate. Welds that have been cleaned hundreds of times, valves that have been cycled thousands of times, pumps that have run for years — this is normal wear and not a reason to avoid a purchase.

What you're looking for is evidence of neglect: deferred maintenance, corrosion that wasn't addressed, repairs that were patched rather than fixed properly. These are signs of how the equipment was operated, and they predict how it will behave going forward.

## Brewhouses: What to Check

**Inside the vessels**: Get inside the kettle and mash tun if possible. Look at the welds at the bottom and sides. Pitting — small pockmarks in the stainless — indicates chemical attack, usually from improper cleaning chemistry or caustic sitting too long. Minor surface pitting is cosmetic. Deep pitting near welds is a structural concern.

**CIP spray balls and ports**: Pull the CIP spray balls and check that the ports are clear and unobstructed. A brewhouse that wasn't CIP'd properly accumulates residue that is difficult to remove and can affect beer quality.

**Heating elements**: On steam systems, look at the steam jacket condition and check that the condensate return system is intact. On direct fire systems, check burner condition and igniter function.

**Control panel**: This is often the most expensive thing to repair on an older brewhouse. Ask for wiring diagrams and confirm that replacement components are still available for the control system in use. A PLC from 2008 may be difficult and expensive to source parts for.

## Fermenters and Bright Tanks: What to Check

**Interior welds**: The weld at the cone is the most stress-prone point on a conical fermenter. Look for cracks, pitting, or evidence of repair.

**Jacket integrity**: Press on the jacket and listen for hollow spots that might indicate a failed weld. Ask whether the jacket has ever been pressure-tested.

**Ports and fittings**: Check every port for thread condition and gasket seating surface. A damaged tri-clamp ferrule needs to be replaced before the tank goes into service.

**Cooling capacity**: If possible, confirm the chilling circuit holds pressure and that the glycol connections are in good condition.

## Canning Lines: What to Check

Canning lines have more moving parts than any other piece of brewery equipment, and condition varies more widely in the secondary market.

**Seamer condition**: The seamer is the heart of the line and the most expensive component to repair. Ask for can seam measurements from a recent production run. A seamer that's been properly maintained will have documentation.

**Filler head condition**: Check filler heads for wear and confirm that rebuild kits are available for the specific model.

**Conveyor and frame**: Look for frame straightness and conveyor wear. These are relatively inexpensive to address but indicate how the line was maintained overall.

**Control systems**: Same principle as brewhouses — confirm that the control system is current enough to source replacement parts.

## The Right Way to Buy Used Equipment

In-person inspection is the standard for high-value equipment purchases. Photos document condition at a point in time; they don't tell you how a system sounds under load or what the inside of a vessel looks like.

If you're buying from a dealer, ask what inspection they performed before listing the equipment and what documentation is available from the prior operation. A reputable dealer can answer these questions specifically.

If you're buying direct from a closing brewery, ask to see maintenance records and talk to the person who ran the equipment. Operators know their systems, and a ten-minute conversation often tells you more than an hour of physical inspection.

The goal isn't to find perfect equipment — that's what new equipment is for. The goal is to find equipment whose condition matches its price, and whose history you understand well enough to make an informed decision.
