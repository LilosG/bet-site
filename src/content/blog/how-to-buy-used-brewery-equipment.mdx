Buying used brewery equipment is one of the highest-leverage decisions a brewery makes. Get it right and you operate at 60 cents on the dollar compared to buying new. Get it wrong and you are troubleshooting someone else's deferred maintenance at 2 a.m. during your first production run.

This guide covers what to inspect, what to ask, and how to think about the full transaction — not just the purchase price.

## Start with Total Installed Cost, Not Sticker Price

The sticker price on a used brewhouse tells you almost nothing. What matters is total installed cost: equipment price plus freight, rigging, de-installation at the source facility, installation at your facility, utilities connection, and startup commissioning.

A 30 BBL brewhouse listed at $75,000 can easily run $110,000 by the time it is producing. A buyer who budgets only the equipment price will be short — sometimes by enough to threaten the project.

**Before making an offer, get a rigging quote.** A competent dealer will give you a scope-of-work estimate covering load-out, freight, and installation as a package. If they cannot, that is a signal about how the rest of the transaction will go.

## Condition Grading: What It Actually Means

Condition descriptions in equipment listings range from precise to meaningless. Here is what to look for in documentation:

- **Photographs** — Interior and exterior. Welds, ports, tri-clamp fittings, glycol jackets, insulation condition. Any listing without interior photography of a vessel is incomplete.
- **Manufacturer and year** — Reputable manufacturers (DME, Marks, Specific Mechanical, Criveller, Prospero) have parts availability and service histories. Generic Chinese-manufactured systems may not.
- **Last use date** — Equipment that sat idle for 18 months in a closed facility is different from equipment that was in weekly production last month.
- **Reason for sale** — Upgrade, closure, consolidation, or financial distress each carry different implications for condition and price negotiability.

## The In-Person Inspection

For any purchase over $15,000, inspect in person before committing. During inspection:

**Vessels (brewhouses, fermenters, brite tanks):**
- Inspect all welds — look for pitting, corrosion, or repairs
- Check all valves, ports, and fittings for leaks or wear
- Inspect insulation condition on jacketed vessels
- Confirm glycol jacket integrity — pressure test if possible
- Look for dents, deformation, or evidence of impact damage

**Mechanical systems (pumps, chillers, boilers):**
- Ask for operating hours where available
- Listen for abnormal sounds during operation if the system can be energized
- Inspect electrical connections and control panels for condition
- Confirm refrigerant type on chillers — some older units use refrigerants that are no longer available

**Canning and packaging lines:**
- Confirm format compatibility with your can sizes
- Check fill head condition — replacement parts can be expensive and slow to source
- Ask about the last seamer calibration and who performed it

## Pricing Benchmarks

Used equipment pricing varies widely based on manufacturer, condition, size, and market timing. General benchmarks as of 2026:

- **7–15 BBL brewhouse systems:** $18,000–$55,000 depending on manufacturer and configuration
- **30 BBL+ systems:** $55,000–$120,000 for premium manufacturer systems in good condition
- **Conical fermenters (per vessel, 30–60 BBL):** $8,000–$22,000
- **Canning lines (semi-auto to automatic):** $25,000–$85,000
- **Glycol chillers (10–40 ton):** $8,000–$28,000

These ranges assume equipment in good working condition from identifiable manufacturers. Off-brand or unknown-condition equipment trades at significant discounts for good reason.

## Rigging and Installation: Budget It Before You Commit

Rigging is not optional. A 30 BBL brewhouse weighs several tons and requires crane equipment, flatbed freight, and skilled millwright labor to move and install safely. Attempting to source rigging separately from the equipment purchase introduces coordination risk and accountability gaps.

The most efficient approach is to source equipment from a dealer who handles the full scope: de-installation, freight, delivery, set and level, utilities rough-in coordination, and startup commissioning. One point of contact, one quote, one scope of accountability.

If you are buying privately (not through a dealer), budget 15–25% of equipment cost for rigging and installation as a conservative estimate. Get a firm quote before you commit.

## What to Ask the Seller

Before making an offer on any major piece of equipment, get answers to these questions:

1. Who manufactured it, and when?
2. What was it last used for, and when was it last in operation?
3. Has it had any repairs, modifications, or component replacements?
4. Is documentation available — manuals, maintenance records, prior inspection reports?
5. Can it be inspected in person before purchase?
6. Is rigging and load-out included, or is that the buyer's responsibility?

A seller who cannot or will not answer these questions is telling you something.

## Working With a Dealer vs. Buying Privately

Buying directly from a brewery that is closing or upgrading can produce good prices. It also transfers all risk — condition, rigging, transportation — entirely to the buyer. You are buying as-is from someone whose expertise is running a brewery, not selling equipment.

A dealer who specializes in used brewery equipment provides:
- Pre-listing inspection and condition documentation
- Consistent pricing based on actual market knowledge
- Rigging and logistics coordination
- Accountability through the full transaction

The price difference between dealer and private is often smaller than buyers expect, and the risk difference is substantial.
