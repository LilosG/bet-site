Sizing a brewhouse is one of the most consequential equipment decisions a brewery makes. Get it wrong in either direction and you pay for it every week — either in brew day frequency or in carrying costs on capacity you can't fill.

## Start With Your Weekly Volume Target, Not Your Current Volume

The most common mistake new breweries make is sizing for current demand. By the time a brewhouse is ordered, delivered, installed, and commissioned, six to twelve months have passed. Size for where you expect to be eighteen months after opening, not where you are today.

A useful starting framework: take your projected weekly barrel output at eighteen months and divide by the number of brew days you want to run per week. That gives you your target batch size.

If you're projecting 60 BBL per week at two brew days, you need a 30 BBL system. If you want to run four brew days, a 15 BBL system works. The brew day frequency is a business decision, not just an equipment decision — more brew days means more labor, more cleaning cycles, more utilities.

## Vessel Configuration Matters as Much as Size

A 15 BBL brewhouse can mean a 2-vessel system or a 3-vessel system, and the operational difference is significant.

A 2-vessel system — typically combined mash/lauter tun and kettle/whirlpool — is more compact and less expensive, but limits your ability to overlap brew days. You finish one batch before starting the next.

A 3-vessel system separates the mash/lauter tun from the kettle, allowing you to start mashing the next batch while the first is still boiling. For high-volume operations, this dramatically increases throughput without adding floor space.

Most production breweries running more than two brew days per week should be looking at 3-vessel systems. The additional vessel pays for itself quickly in labor efficiency.

## Heating Method: Direct Fire vs Steam

Direct fire systems — gas burners under the vessels — are simpler to install and have lower upfront cost. They work well for smaller operations and facilities without existing steam infrastructure.

Steam-heated systems require a boiler, but offer more precise temperature control, faster heating, and significantly lower long-term utility costs at scale. For production breweries running multiple brew days per week, steam typically wins on total cost of ownership within two to three years.

If you're buying a used brewhouse, confirm the heating method matches your facility's utility infrastructure before committing. Converting a steam system to direct fire — or the reverse — is expensive.

## Used Brewhouses: What to Verify Before Purchasing

The secondary market for brewhouses is active and well-supplied. Most used systems available today came from working production breweries that upgraded to larger capacity — meaning they were in regular use, not sitting idle.

Key things to verify on any used brewhouse purchase:

- **Vessel condition**: look for pitting, welds, and CIP port condition inside the vessels
- **Pump and valve operation**: these are the components most likely to need attention on a used system
- **Control panel**: older systems may have control panels that need updating or are difficult to source parts for
- **Documentation**: manufacturer specs, wiring diagrams, and any service records are valuable

In-person inspection before purchase is worth the trip. A used brewhouse is a significant capital commitment and the condition of welds, ports, and fittings is difficult to evaluate from photos alone.

## The Real Cost of a Brewhouse Is Not Just the Purchase Price

Factor in rigging and installation before you finalize a budget. A brewhouse doesn't move itself. De-installation at the source, freight, delivery, set and level at your facility, utilities connection, and commissioning are all real costs that need to be in your project budget before you sign a purchase agreement.

A rough rule: budget 15 to 25 percent of the equipment purchase price for rigging and installation on a used brewhouse, depending on complexity and distance. Get a firm quote before you buy.

The right system at the right price, properly installed, is what matters. The purchase price is only part of the equation.
