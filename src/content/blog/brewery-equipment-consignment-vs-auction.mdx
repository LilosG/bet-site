When a brewery needs to liquidate equipment — whether from a closure, a capacity upgrade, or a facility consolidation — the method matters as much as the market. Consignment and auction serve different situations, and choosing the wrong one costs you either time or recovery value.

## The Core Tradeoff

Consignment optimizes for recovery value. Auction optimizes for speed and certainty of sale.

Neither is universally better. The right choice depends on your timeline, the equipment you have, and how much you need to recover versus how quickly you need liquidity.

## When Consignment Is the Right Choice

Consignment works best when you have time and high-value equipment.

A production brewhouse, a full canning line, or a glycol system that's in good condition and priced correctly will sell — it just may take thirty to ninety days to reach the right buyer. The consignment process involves professional photography, detailed listing creation, and marketing to a qualified buyer network. That effort produces a better outcome than a rushed sale.

Consignment is particularly well-suited for:

- **Capacity upgrades**: you're replacing a 15 BBL system with a 30 BBL system. The outgoing equipment has real value and there's no urgency — you just need it gone before the new system arrives.
- **Partial liquidations**: you're selling specific pieces, not the whole facility. Individual high-value items move well on consignment.
- **Partnership dissolutions**: a structured process with documented proceeds and timeline works better for multiple stakeholders.

The key variable is timeline. If you have sixty to ninety days, consignment almost always produces better recovery than a forced auction.

## When Auction Is the Right Choice

Auction is designed for speed and defined timelines.

If a lease is ending, a lender is involved, or a closure needs to happen on a specific date, auction provides certainty. Equipment sells within a defined window. The event creates competitive pressure among buyers that can drive prices higher than expected on in-demand items — particularly brewhouses and canning lines.

Auction works best when:

- **Timeline is fixed**: you have a hard date by which the facility must be cleared
- **Full facility liquidation**: liquidating an entire brewery at once is complex to manage piece by piece; an auction event handles it as a single coordinated process
- **Lender or investor recovery**: investors and lenders recovering assets from a distressed situation typically prefer the speed and documentation of an auction

The downside of auction is that it sets a floor, not a ceiling, on recovery. Equipment that would command a premium through consignment over ninety days may sell below that level in a competitive auction event. For some situations that's an acceptable tradeoff.

## The Hybrid Approach

For full facility liquidations, a hybrid approach often makes sense: consign the highest-value items individually while auctioning the balance of the facility.

A well-maintained 20 BBL brewhouse may warrant individual consignment listing to reach the right buyer at full market value. The remaining fermenters, kegs, small equipment, and supporting systems can be bundled into an auction event that clears the facility on a defined timeline.

This approach maximizes recovery on the items that have the most upside while providing timeline certainty on the rest.

## What to Ask Before You Decide

Before committing to either approach, get clear answers on:

- What's your hard deadline for clearing the facility?
- Which pieces have the highest individual market value?
- Are there any liens on the equipment that need to be addressed?
- What's the minimum recovery you need to cover your obligations?

Those answers will determine the right approach more than any general rule about consignment versus auction.
